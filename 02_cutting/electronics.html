Use Ohm's law to calculate the current flowing through a resistor. ![pic](./electronics/Current-OhmsLawDiagram.png) - An LED does not have the simple relation between voltage and current that a resistor does (Ohm's law does not work for diodes). To learn what current is flowing through the LED, you need to use the fact that the current flowing through the resistor is the same as that flowing through the LED. Then measure the voltage across the resistor and use Ohm's Law to find the current. Build the circuit shown above, and calculate the current flowing through the circuit. --- ##Kirchhoff's Circuit Laws [Kirchhoff's current law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirchhoff%27s_circuit_laws) states that, for any node (junction) in an electrical circuit, the sum of currents flowing into that node is equal to the sum of currents flowing out of that node. ![pic](./electronics/KCL.png) The current entering any junction is equal to the current leaving that junction. _i2 + i3 = i1 + i4_ Kirchhoff's Voltage Rule states that the directed sum of the potential differences (voltages) around any closed loop is zero. ![pic](./electronics/KVL.png) _v1 + v2 + v3 + v4 = 0_ --- ##Resistance ![pic](./electronics/series.png) --- ##Arduino as a voltmeter ![pic](./electronics/voltmeter.jpg) Use the Arduino as a voltmeter. It is calculated as: <b> P = I x V </b> With current in Amperes and V in volts, Power has units of Joules/second or Watts. Alternatively, Ohm's Law can be used to give: <b>P=I<sup>2</sup>R or P=V<sup>2</sup>/R </b> --- ##Voltage Divider ![pic](./electronics/VoltageDivider.png) - Voltage divider.
